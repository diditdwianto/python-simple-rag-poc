# Coffee Brewing & Storage Guide (Indo-Trader-Inc)

## Indonesian coffee characteristics
Most Indonesian coffees are processed using the wet-hulled method (Giling Basah),
which is common in Sumatra and Sulawesi. This process gives the coffee a heavy
body, low acidity, and earthy or herbal flavors that distinguish Indonesian beans
from the brighter, fruitier coffees of Africa or Central America.

## Recommended brew ratios
A good general starting ratio is 1 gram of coffee to 16 grams of water (1:16).
For a stronger cup use 1:15; for a lighter cup use 1:17.

## Brewing methods
- Pour-over (V60, Kalita): use a medium-fine grind, water at 92–96°C, brew time
  2.5–3.5 minutes. Best for highlighting Bali Kintamani and Aceh Gayo.
- French press: use a coarse grind, 4 minute steep, then press. Great for the
  full body of Sumatra Mandheling and Toraja.
- Espresso: use a fine grind. Sumatra Mandheling and Toraja make rich, low-acid
  espresso and pair well with milk.
- Cold brew: use a coarse grind, steep in cold water for 12–18 hours, ratio 1:8,
  then dilute to taste.

## Grind size quick reference
- Coarse: French press, cold brew
- Medium: drip / pour-over
- Fine: espresso
- Extra fine: Turkish

## Storage
Store beans in an airtight container away from light, heat, and moisture. Do not
refrigerate or freeze opened coffee, as condensation harms the beans. For best
flavor, use beans within 2–4 weeks of the roast date and grind just before
brewing. Indo-Trader-Inc prints the roast date on every bag.

## Caffeine note
Darker roasts like Sumatra Mandheling do not contain more caffeine than lighter
roasts; by weight the difference is negligible. A typical 250ml cup brewed at a
1:16 ratio contains roughly 95–120 mg of caffeine.
